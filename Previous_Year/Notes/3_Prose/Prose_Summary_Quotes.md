* **Start → Climax → End:**

* **Key Ideas:**

  * Friendship eases grief and doubles joy
  * A friend helps in clear thinking and decision-making
  * True friends give honest advice

* **Quote:**

  > “A principal fruit of friendship is the ease and discharge of the fulness and swellings of the heart.”

### 3️⃣ Charles Lamb – *Christ's Hospital Five and Thirty Years Ago* (D)

**Theme:** School life, childhood memories, discipline, injustice

**Main Characters:**

* Charles Lamb (Elia)
* George Dyer
* James Boyer (Headmaster)

**Start → Climax → End:**
Lamb recalls his school days at Christ’s Hospital → Describes strict discipline and harsh treatment of some students → Ends with reflection on how experiences shaped character and friendship

**Important Points:**

* The essay is autobiographical.
* Lamb compares past and present conditions of the school.
* He praises good teachers but criticizes cruelty and favoritism.
* Shows sympathy for poor students.

## Important Quotes

### 1. **“I remember L. at school, and I was a poor, friendless boy.”**

* **Who said it:** This is Charles Lamb himself, as a **first-person narrator**.
* **What it means:** Lamb is recalling his own childhood experience at **Christ’s Hospital**, the school he attended. He emphasizes his **loneliness and vulnerability**, highlighting that he had no close friends and felt socially isolated.
* **Who he referred to:** He is referring to **himself**, reflecting on his early experiences.

### 2. **“We were suffered to suffer all of these.”**

* **Who said it:** Again, Lamb as the **narrator**.
* **What it means:** He’s commenting on the **harsh discipline and strict regime** of Christ’s Hospital. “Suffered to suffer” means that the boys endured the strict rules, punishments, or hardships imposed on them without complaint, almost as if the adults expected them to endure.
* **Who he referred to:** Lamb is speaking about **himself and his fellow schoolmates**—all the children under the school’s strict control.

### 3. **“I remember L. at school…”**

* **Who said it:** Charles Lamb (the narrator)

* **Who “L.” is:** A friend and fellow student at Christ’s Hospital, referring to Samuel Taylor Coleridge. Lamb uses the initial “L.” as a literary device, which was common in essays of that time, although readers later understood it referred to Samuel Taylor Coleridge.

* **Meaning:** Lamb is recalling his schooldays and describing Coleridge’s special privileges, personality, and intellectual abilities. He uses these memories to contrast different student experiences and to show admiration for Coleridge’s intelligence.

### 4. **General context of these lines:**

* Charles Lamb often writes in a **personal, nostalgic style**, blending humor with subtle criticism.
* The “friendless boy” and “we were suffered to suffer” lines show **childhood hardships** but also a sort of **bond among students**, as they all endured the strict schooling together.
* The essay is both **a memoir and a social commentary**, reflecting on the strictness of old English schools and the emotional life of children within them.

## ⭐ 1.

> **“For if you suffer your people to be ill-educated and their manners to be corrupted from their infancy… what else is to be concluded from this but that you first make thieves and then punish them?”**

### ✅ Explanation:

More criticizes the harsh punishment of criminals.
He argues that society itself creates crime through poverty and lack of education. Instead of punishing thieves, the government should remove social injustice and provide proper education and employment.

---

## ⭐ 2.

> **“In Utopia, where everything belongs to everyone, no one has anything.”**

### ✅ Explanation:

This reflects the idea of **common ownership of property**.
Private property is abolished in Utopia. More suggests that greed and inequality come from private ownership, and communal living ensures equality.

---

## ⭐ 3.

> **“Where there is private property and money is the standard of all things, it is hardly possible for a commonwealth to be governed justly.”**

### ✅ Explanation:

More criticizes capitalism and wealth inequality.
He believes money and private property lead to corruption, greed, and injustice. A just society must focus on equality rather than wealth.

---

## ⭐ 4.

> **“A man would rather choose to be merry than wise.”**

### ✅ Explanation:

This shows human nature.
People often prefer pleasure over wisdom. More gently mocks human weakness and suggests that ideal society requires discipline and rational thinking.

---

## ⭐ 5.

> **“No man ought to be punished for his religion.”**

### ✅ Explanation:

This reflects More’s idea of **religious tolerance** in Utopia.
Although he lived during religious conflicts, in *Utopia* he supports freedom of belief (with some limits). This shows Renaissance humanist thinking.
