# Age of Shakespeare

# 1️⃣ The Renaissance and its Influence on Elizabethan Literature

### 🔹 Meaning of Renaissance

* Renaissance means **“rebirth of learning”**.
* It was a revival of **classical Greek and Roman knowledge, art, and literature**.
* It began in Italy and later spread to England in the 16th century.

---

### 🔹 Influence on Elizabethan Literature

**1️⃣ Growth of Humanism**

* Focus shifted from religion to **human life, human emotions, and individual potential**.
* Writers explored themes like **love, beauty, ambition, and human nature**.

Example: William Shakespeare wrote about human emotions and psychology.

---

**2️⃣ Development of New Literary Forms**

* New forms like **sonnets, essays, dramas, and prose** developed.
* Writers experimented with new styles and techniques.

Example: Francis Bacon developed the essay form.

---

**3️⃣ Influence of Classical Literature**

* Writers were inspired by **Greek and Roman writers** like Homer and Virgil.
* They followed classical ideas of **order, beauty, and balance**.

This improved the quality and structure of English literature.

---

**4️⃣ Rise of Drama**

* Drama became very popular during the Elizabethan Age.
* Many great plays were written and performed in theatres.

Example: Shakespeare’s tragedies, comedies, and histories.

---

**5️⃣ Development of English Language**

* English language became richer and more expressive.
* Many new words and expressions were introduced.

Writers began using English instead of Latin.

---

**6️⃣ Spirit of Curiosity and Exploration**

* Renaissance encouraged **scientific thinking, discovery, and creativity**.
* Writers explored new ideas about **life, nature, and the universe**.

---

### 🔹 Conclusion

The Renaissance greatly influenced Elizabethan literature by promoting humanism, classical learning, and creativity. It led to the growth of drama, prose, and poetry, and produced great writers like Shakespeare and Francis Bacon.

---

# 2️⃣ University Wits & Pre-Shakespearean Drama

These writers developed English drama before Shakespeare.

The **University Wits** were a group of **highly educated English playwrights of the late 16th century (about 1580–1595)** 🎓, who studied at Oxford and Cambridge and helped transform English drama into a mature literary form. They existed in the **late Elizabethan period**, just before and during the early career of William Shakespeare, and long before the **Age of John Milton (1625–1660)**. They were called “wits” because of their intelligence and literary talent. Writers like Christopher Marlowe, Thomas Kyd, and Robert Greene improved drama by introducing **blank verse, strong plots, tragic heroes, and artistic structure** 🎭. This created a clear literary chain of development: first, the University Wits **refined drama**, then Shakespeare **perfected drama**, and later Milton **shifted focus to epic poetry and serious religious literature**, especially with *Paradise Lost*. Thus, the University Wits form an important **foundation stage in the evolution of English literature leading from early drama to Shakespeare’s greatness and finally to Milton’s epic and Puritan literature**.

# 4️⃣ Development of Prose

Prose became powerful and influential.

## 📖 Development of Prose in the Latter Half of the 16th Century

The latter half of the 16th century (1550–1600) is part of the **Elizabethan Age**, ruled by Elizabeth I. This period saw a major growth in English prose due to education, Renaissance influence, and printing.

---

## 1. Reasons for the Development of Prose

### 🔹 Renaissance influence

The Renaissance encouraged:

* Learning
* Curiosity
* Scientific thinking
* Interest in classical literature

Writers began expressing ideas in prose for clarity.

---

### 🔹 Spread of education

More people became educated and literate.

This increased:

* Reading
* Writing
* Demand for prose works

---

### 🔹 Printing press influence

The printing press, introduced by William Caxton, helped in:

* Publishing books
* Spreading knowledge
* Making prose popular

---

### 🔹 Religious and political changes

The Reformation created religious debates.

People used prose to express:

* Religious ideas
* Political opinions

---

## 2. Characteristics of Elizabethan Prose

Elizabethan prose was:

* Elaborate and decorative
* Influenced by classical writers
* Intellectual and scholarly
* Sometimes artificial and ornamental

Later, prose became simpler and clearer.

---

## 3. Major Prose Writers and Their Contributions

---

### 📘 Francis Bacon

Francis Bacon

**Major work:**

* Essays

**Contribution:**

* Known as the Father of English Prose
* Developed the essay as a literary form
* His prose is concise, clear, and full of wisdom

He wrote on:

* Truth
* Studies
* Friendship
* Ambition

---

### 📘 John Lyly

John Lyly

**Major work:**

* Euphues: The Anatomy of Wit

**Contribution:**

* Introduced a prose style called *Euphuism*
* Style was artificial, decorative, and balanced

Features:

* Use of similes
* Parallel sentences
* Mythological references

---

### 📘 Richard Hooker

Richard Hooker

**Major work:**

* Of the Laws of Ecclesiastical Polity

**Contribution:**

* Wrote religious and philosophical prose
* His prose is clear, logical, and balanced

---

## 4. Types of Prose Written

Main prose forms included:

* Essays
* Religious prose
* Philosophical prose
* Prose romances

---

## 5. Importance of Prose Development

This period was important because:

* English prose became mature
* Essay developed as a literary form
* Prose became a powerful medium of expression
* Foundation was laid for modern prose

---

## 6. Conclusion

The latter half of the 16th century saw great development in English prose due to Renaissance influence, education, and printing. Writers like Francis Bacon, John Lyly, and Richard Hooker made important contributions. Bacon developed the essay form, Lyly introduced Euphuism, and Hooker wrote clear religious prose. This period laid the foundation for modern English prose.a
